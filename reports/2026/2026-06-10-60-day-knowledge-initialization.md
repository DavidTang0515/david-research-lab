## 2. Economy

### What happened?

Global growth signals remain mixed. Major institutions and market commentary continue to point to slower growth, sticky inflation risks, interest-rate uncertainty and weaker confidence in trade-heavy sectors. AI-related capital expenditure is a visible support for investment, but it does not remove pressure from energy, financing costs or policy uncertainty.

### Why does it matter?

For enterprises, the macro environment is not a simple recovery story. Capital is still selective. Projects with unclear payback periods will face more scrutiny. Cost control, supply-chain resilience and cash-flow discipline remain important.

### Long-term knowledge to retain

- AI capital expenditure can support parts of the economy while leaving household consumption and traditional sectors weak.
- Interest rates affect digital transformation because they change the acceptable payback period for infrastructure projects.
- Inflation risk increasingly comes from supply chains, energy and strategic commodities, not only demand.

## 3. Technology and AI

### What happened?

AI attention is shifting from model releases to practical deployment: agents, enterprise workflows, compute infrastructure, pricing and measurable productivity. The market is asking whether AI can move from experimentation to repeatable business value.

### Why does it matter?

The next stage of AI adoption will not be decided only by model capability. It will be decided by reliability, permission control, cost per task, workflow fit and data governance. Enterprises that only buy tools may not capture much value.

### Long-term knowledge to retain

- Agentic AI is a workflow technology, not just a chatbot upgrade.
- The value of AI depends on integration with business systems, data permissions and audit trails.
- AI cost management will become a normal part of enterprise budgeting.

## 4. Compute Infrastructure and Semiconductors

### What happened?

AI data-center buildout continues to drive demand for accelerators, networking, memory, storage, power and advanced packaging. Memory and supply-chain constraints are becoming broader industrial issues, not only semiconductor-company issues.

### Why does it matter?

Compute is becoming the physical base of the AI economy. Shortages or price increases in memory and chips can spill over into consumer electronics, cloud pricing, enterprise IT budgets and national industrial policy.

### Long-term knowledge to retain

- AI infrastructure is not only GPU supply; it includes memory, power, networking, cooling and financing.
- Semiconductor bottlenecks can transfer costs from AI companies to downstream industries.
- Control over compute capacity is becoming a geopolitical and industrial bargaining chip.

## 5. Politics and Geopolitics

### What happened?

Technology competition remains central to China-U.S. relations. Export controls, domestic substitution, critical minerals, cloud infrastructure and AI sovereignty are now part of the same policy field. Europe is also increasing attention to digital sovereignty and reducing dependency on foreign technology stacks.

### Why does it matter?

Technology choices are no longer purely commercial. Vendor selection, cloud deployment, chip access and data location can become political and compliance issues. Large organizations must treat technology procurement as strategic risk management.

### Long-term knowledge to retain

- Export controls may slow access to advanced technology while also accelerating local alternatives.
- Digital sovereignty is a long-term policy theme for Europe and other major economies.
- Supply-chain resilience increasingly means political resilience.

## 6. Cybersecurity

### What happened?

AI is changing the security landscape. It improves defense automation but also expands attack paths through data leakage, identity abuse, social engineering, model misuse and autonomous tool execution.

### Why does it matter?

Large organizations cannot treat AI tools as isolated software. Once AI systems can read files, call tools, summarize internal data or act on behalf of employees, the security boundary moves into permissions, data classification and auditability.

### Long-term knowledge to retain

- AI security is partly cybersecurity, partly data governance and partly operational control.
- Identity and permission design become more important when agents can act across systems.
- Security review should happen before workflow automation, not after incidents.

---

## 7. Enterprise Digital Transformation

### What happened?

Enterprise digitalization is moving from building systems to extracting value from systems. The new question is not whether an organization has cloud, data platforms or AI tools, but whether these assets improve decisions, reduce friction and create measurable operating results.

### Why does it matter for SOEs and large organizations?

State-owned enterprises and large organizations usually have complex approval chains, legacy systems, regulatory requirements and fragmented data ownership. AI can improve productivity only if these management constraints are addressed.

### Long-term knowledge to retain

- Digital transformation should start from business processes, not tool procurement.
- Data ownership and responsibility must be clear before AI can scale safely.
- SOE digitalization should emphasize compliance, security, continuity and measurable public-service or operational value.
